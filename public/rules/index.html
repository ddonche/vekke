Vekke Overview

By Intl. Vekke Council ~ 10 min read

Vekke (to awaken in Norwegian) is a language-independent abstract strategy game played using universal symbols. Two players compete to eliminate or lock all opposing units (tokens) from the board using a fixed movement language of 24 routes—predefined pairings of direction and distance. Players maneuver tokens through capture and siege, with victory achieved by locking or removing all opposing tokens from play (there are others, but these are the primary ones).

Unlike many maneuver-based strategy games, Vekke features flanking, allowing tokens to traverse beyond the board edge and re-enter from the opposite side. This creates continuous spatial pressure and prevents static end states. As a result, Vekke does not permit draws: every match resolves decisively.

With the exception of optional wildcards (used in certain formats), Vekke contains no random elements. All outcomes arise from player choice, foresight, and positional judgment.

Quick Overview:

  • Players: 2
  • Duration: 15–45 minutes
  • Complexity: Medium
  • Components: Symbol-based (no text)
  • Win Condition: Multiple (elimination and siegemate primary)

Win Conditions

Vekke ends decisively—no draws possible due to flanking and economy pressure.1

Condition Description
Elimination Remove all enemy units from play via capture or Perfect Siege.
Siegemate All enemy units under siege with no reserves left.
Collapse Opponent has no legal moves and no reserves (self-inflicted).2
Resignation Opponent concedes.
Timeout Opponent exceeds time limit.3
Note
No draws in Vekke: flanking lets units wrap around board edges to prevent stalemates, while visible routes and reserves create constant pressure until one player eliminates or locks out the opponent.

History

Vekke was first developed around 2014 as a supplemental system for an unreleased mystery-style game. 4 In its original conception, the game’s movement system—based on fixed routes—was intended to function as a cipher. Players of the mystery game were expected to discover the actual game rules online and apply them as part of the larger puzzle structure.

When the mystery project did not come to fruition, development continued independently on the abstract strategy game itself. During this period, the game was known as Betaori, a reference to Beta Orionis (Rigel), the brightest star in the Orion constellation. 5 Rigel’s blue-white coloration directly informed the game’s use of blue and white tokens, which are still the default in each set.

Prior to final prototyping in 2026, the game was renamed Vekke. The change was made to avoid confusion with the Japanese mahjong term betaori and to establish a standalone identity. The name Vekke—meaning “to awaken” in Norwegian—was selected to reflect the game’s emphasis on insight, discovery, and deliberate play. 6 At the time of renaming, the domain associated with the name was already under the creator’s control and unused.

Lore
Following the name change, it was also decided that players would be allowed to use custom "gear" (tokens and routes, boards, etc). This meant not everyone would be using white and blue tokens for units. Since the website had already coded player sides as W and B, names of the sides changed from "white" and "blue" to "wake" and "brake". Wake was chosen because of Vekke's Norwegian meaning, and since W plays first (awakens), brake was chosen to represent the counter-balance to this.

Components and Board

Vekke intersection-style board

Vekke is played on a 6×6 grid, which may be represented either as square spaces or as intersecting lines with placement points at each intersection. Players sit on opposite sides of the board. Originally, one player used white tokens, and the other used blue tokens. Now, any color or material may be used, with one side being wake and the other being brake. The board still indicates which is which with blue or white edges.

Unit tokens are typically flat stones (such as glass or resin pieces similar to those used in Go), though some sets may use checkers-style pieces instead. Players take turns using their available routes to maneuver units. Play continues until one player has eliminated or locked all opposing units from the board.

List of Components

A standard Vekke game set consists of the following components:

Core Components:

  • game board
  • 18 white unit tokens
  • 18 blue unit tokens
  • 28 route markers (cards, tiles, or dominoes)
  • 3 reference cards
  • rule booklet
  • box

Optional / Format-Specific:

  • 12 wildcards
  • 8 direction markers (cards or tiles)

Core Principles

The mechanics and concept of the game are meant to be simple. Identical units. Use routes to move them. Capture or siege. That's the core concept. That's Standard Format. Of course, there is endless depth once you add token economy into the mix, which is Tournament Format.

Movement and notation in Vekke rely on a purely symbolic system rather than written language. Direction and distance are represented visually, allowing the game to be played without translation and understood across cultures (for the components; rules will still need to be translated). This symbolic foundation supports international competition, accessibility, and consistent interpretation in both casual and tournament play.

Balance

Vekke is designed to become progressively more intense as play advances. Each player begins with three unit tokens in play, with the remaining tokens held in the reserves. As the game unfolds, players introduce additional units through reinforcements, increasing board density and tactical pressure over time.

To offset the structural advantage of being first to place the opening unit (B brake), the W wake player is granted first move. Balance is further reinforced by full route visibility: each player’s available routes are kept in view, allowing both sides to anticipate potential maneuvers and plan accordingly. Routes in the queue are also visible.

Perfect Information
In game design, having all information available to all players is called "perfect information." 7

Players may optionally introduce directional randomization by using Explorer format, which alters orientation while preserving the underlying movement system.

Rewarding Skill

Vekke's movement via routes to achieve capture or siege is only part of the tactical skill involved. The other half of the game requires timing and decision regarding token economy. Do you spend reserves to buy an extra reinforcement? Do you spend captives to use an early route swap or do you hold them to get reserves back? Can you time a recoil to disrupt your opponent? Do you sacrifice a live unit (defection) to prevent your opponent from drafting? Do you preoccupy your units by holding a siege or do you abandon it to get a capture?

These are all questions you will think to yourself.

Vekke is also structured so that higher-difficulty maneuvers yield greater rewards than simpler plays. While a basic ransom can return 1 unit from the void, one can return 2 units via the more complex drafting, which demands additional coordination and positional foresight.

Tournament Play

Vekke was designed with organized competition in mind. As a result, official tournaments use a single standardized ruleset known as Tournament Format, commonly referred to as escalating.

Tournament Format differs from other formats in several key ways. Directions are fixed, with direction 8 defined as northwest from the B side. Wildcards are not used. Special Actions are used.

This structure ensures consistency, eliminates ambiguity, and allows competitive play to scale in complexity as a match progresses.

Identity Through Order

Vekke also includes 9 different factions called Orders. These are purely aesthetic and philosophical; there is no competitive advantage. The rules remain the same for all. These Orders represent various styles of play, and each comes with an animal sigil, color scheme, banner, and order-specific gear set. You may join any order at any time, and you may leave at any time.
