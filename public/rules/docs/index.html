Vekke Overview

By Intl. Vekke Council ~ 8 min read

Vekke (to awaken in Norwegian) is a language-independent abstract strategy game played using universal symbols. Two players compete to eliminate all opposing tokens from the board using a fixed movement language of 24 routes—predefined pairings of direction and distance. Players maneuver tokens through capture and siege, with victory achieved by removing all opposing tokens from play.

Unlike many maneuver-based strategy games, Vekke features flanking, allowing tokens to traverse beyond the board edge and re-enter from the opposite side. This creates continuous spatial pressure and prevents static end states. As a result, Vekke does not permit draws: every match resolves decisively.

With the exception of optional wildcards (used in certain formats), Vekke contains no random elements. All outcomes arise from player choice, foresight, and positional judgment.

Quick Overview:

  • Players: 2
  • Duration: 15–45 minutes
  • Complexity: Medium
  • Components: Symbol-based (no text)
  • Win Condition: Eliminate all opponent tokens

History

Vekke was first developed around 2015 as a supplemental system for an unreleased mystery-style game. In its original conception, the game’s movement system—based on fixed routes—was intended to function as a cipher. Players of the mystery game were expected to discover the rules online and apply them as part of the larger puzzle structure.

When the mystery project did not come to fruition, development continued independently on the abstract strategy game itself. During this period, the game was known as Betaori, a reference to Beta Orionis (Rigel), the brightest star in the Orion constellation. Rigel’s blue-white coloration directly informed the game’s use of blue and white tokens.

Prior to final prototyping in 2026, the game was renamed Vekke. The change was made to avoid confusion with the Japanese mahjong term betaori and to establish a standalone identity. The name Vekke—meaning “to awaken” in Norwegian—was selected to reflect the game’s emphasis on insight, discovery, and deliberate play. At the time of renaming, the domain associated with the name was already under the creator’s control and unused.

Components and Board

Vekke intersection-style board

Vekke is played on a 6×6 grid, which may be represented either as square spaces or as intersecting lines with placement points at each intersection. Players sit on opposite sides of the board. One player uses white tokens, and the other uses blue tokens.

Tokens are typically flat stones (such as glass or resin pieces similar to those used in Go), though some sets may use checkers-style pieces instead. Players take turns using their available routes to maneuver tokens. Play continues until one player has eliminated all opposing tokens from the board.

List of Components

A standard Vekke game set consists of the following components:

Core Components:

  • game board
  • 18 white tokens
  • 18 blue tokens
  • 24 route markers (cards, tiles, or dominoes)
  • 8 direction markers (cards or tiles)

Optional / Format-Specific:

  • 12 wildcards
  • 3 reference cards
  • rule booklet
  • box

Core Principles

Movement and notation in Vekke rely on a purely symbolic system rather than written language. Direction and distance are represented visually, allowing the game to be played without translation and understood across cultures. This symbolic foundation supports international competition, accessibility, and consistent interpretation in both casual and tournament play.

Balance

Vekke is designed to become progressively more intense as play advances. In the standard format, each player begins with four tokens in play, with the remaining tokens held in the token queue. As the game unfolds, players may introduce additional tokens through reinforcements, increasing board density and tactical pressure over time.

To offset the structural advantage of moving first and selecting an initial formation, the blue player is granted first selection of routes during setup. Balance is further reinforced by full route visibility: each player’s available routes are kept in view, allowing both sides to anticipate potential maneuvers and plan accordingly. The sole exception is the hidden route wildcard, which is intentionally concealed.

Players may optionally introduce directional randomization by using Explorer format, which alters orientation while preserving the underlying movement system.

Rewarding Skill

Vekke is structured so that higher-difficulty maneuvers yield greater rewards than simpler plays. While a basic siege requires surrounding an opposing token on four sides, more complex sieges demand additional coordination and positional foresight.

A standard four-sided siege enables the attacking player to earn a route swap. If a siege is completed using five or six tokens, the attacking player is additionally rewarded with extra queue token placements for that turn. These enhanced rewards reflect the increased difficulty and risk involved in executing larger, more precise formations.

Tournament Play

Vekke was designed with organized competition in mind. As a result, official tournaments use a single standardized ruleset known as Tournament Format, commonly referred to as escalating.

Tournament Format differs from other formats in several key ways. Directions are fixed, with direction 8 defined as northwest from the blue side. Wildcards are not used. Each player begins with a single route and gains one additional random route at the start of each round, up to a maximum of four routes. In championship matches, this route cap is removed.

This structure ensures consistency, eliminates ambiguity, and allows competitive play to scale in complexity as a match progresses.

For full gameplay details, see the official Rules.
