Notation

By Intl. Vekke Council ~ 9 min read

Notation is a special syntax or shorthand way for players to keep track of actions during a game. It is somewhat similar to chess notation1, with a few key differences. It should also be noted that the notation players use is different from VGN (Vekke Game Notation), which is the special machine-friendly way the https://vekke.net website keeps track of games. It is also different from the human-friendly log entries you will see in the game.

In general, Vekke notation records the movement of tokens or routes from one place to another. Once you understand that every action is simply a transfer between areas (board, reserves, captives, void, or routes), the notation becomes easy to read.

Note
Chess notation is notably simpler than Vekke notation because in chess, each player performs only one move per turn. In Vekke, several actions are taken each turn; at a minimum there are 5 actions a player takes: using 3 routes to maneuver, placing 1 reinforcement, performing 1 route swap.
Lore
This notation system is meant to be state-agnostic. By this, we meant that we don't specifically say we did a route swap, or that we captured a piece, or that we did a defection. Tokens simply move from place to place. Specific routes are used. This mirrors how Vekke Game Notation functions. We simply track where tokens move.

Notation Legend

  • W — Wake player
  • B — Brake player
  • R — Reserves
  • C — Captives
  • V — Void
  • d1-e1 — token movement
  • 7/2 — route (direction 7, distance 2)
Note
The terms pieces and tokens are used interchangeably here for ease of onboarding.

Tracking Turns Example

Each player's actions are recorded during their turn. Since W (wake) plays first, notation will look like this:

0 Bd4, Wc6, Bf5, Wc4, Bd1, We3
1 W 7/2 e3-c3, 1/1 c4-c5, 4/3 d5-c6, 1R-f1, 7/2-2/1
1 B 3/1 d1-e1, 5/2 f5-f3, 8/1 d4-c5-C, 2R-V, 1R-d5, 1R-b6, 5/2-4/1

That looks like a mess, but it will make sense in a moment. Let's walk through a few things one step at a time.

Read Left to Right

Since each player takes multiple actions during their turn, we simply track each action one at a time. Opening placement is turn 0 and contains the placements of both players; after that each player's turn will be annotated on its own line. Each action will be recorded in turn, so the turn can be read in sequential order from left to right.

Capital Letters

Capital letters mark several key things:

  • W and B are abbreviations for each player, wake and brake
  • R stands for reserves
  • C stands for captives
  • V stands for void

Note that each capital letter either stands for a player or an area where tokens are kept.

Line Numbers

The first character of every line is the turn number. Opening placement is turn 0, and contains all placements of both players. Thereafter, each player's turn will be written on its own line, starting with wake.

Routes

Routes are shown with direction first, a / character, and distance last. Whenever you use a route to move a unit, annotate the route used first, followed by the unit’s movement.. Example: 7/2 (direction 7, 2 spaces).

Note
If a route is unusable, the route is listed followed by x/x. This means that no movement was taken. Do this for each unusable route.

Movement

Since all pieces in Vekke are identical, we annotate which one moves by its grid coordinates. We list the starting coordinate, followed by the destination coordinate, separated by a - character. Example: d1-e1 means the token on d1 moved to e1.

Important
Grid coordinates use lower case letters.

Annotating Capture

If your movement results in a capture, we simply annotate that as a transfer into another area. Look at this section from B's first turn in the example above: d4-c5-C. B moved from d4 to c5. We can tell this was a capture because that piece then went into captives. That's it. Thus, reading left to right, we see a piece moved from d4 -> c5 and that piece moved from c5 -> captives. You will see that this is the same way we treat the other areas (annotated by capital letters).

Reinforcements

When dealing with the different areas (reserves, captives, void), we simply annotate the movement of pieces from one area to the next. We annotate the number of pieces moved. So the example from W's turn one where 1R-f1 means that 1 token from reserves moved to coordinate f1. This same rule applies no matter how many pieces are moving from one area to another.

See how B did the following: 2R-V, 1R-d5, 1R-b6. This means:

  • two pieces moved from reserves to the void (this means an extra reinforcement was likely purchased)
  • one piece moved from reserves to space d5
  • one piece moved from reserves to space b6

This confirms that an extra reinforcement was purchased (since 2 went out into play).

Route Swap

Finally, we have annotation that looks like this: 5/2-4/1. This means a route swap. The first route is always the route from the player's hand, and the second is always the one from the route queue.

Tip
Think of it like this: you discard a route from your hand and you pick up a route from the queue.

Special Actions

Special actions might seem complicated, but we've already covered two of them above. Simply annotate the number of tokens, where they are moving from, and where they are moving to. If it's a route swap, just list hand route followed by queue route. Here's a list of all special actions and how to annotate them:

  • Extra Reinforcement - two reserves move to the void; two tokens from reserves placed onto the board: 2R-V, 1R-xx, 1R-xx (xx being grid coordinate)
  • Early Route Swap - two captives move to the void; route swap like normal: 2C-V, d/d-d/d (d/d being direction/distance)
  • Ransom - two captives move to the void; one friendly token moves to reserves: 2C-V, 1V-R
  • Defection - one token moves from the board to the void; one enemy token moves to captives: c5-V, 1V-C (see note below)
  • Recoil - one token each from reserves and captives moves to the void; one token in play moves: 1R-V, 1C-V, f4-f3
Note
You don't need to annotate W or B when moving things from the void into captives or reserves; the token "color" is implied. You can follow an entire game using the notation alone—based on whose turn it is, you can deduce ownership. (see the example below)

Assume it is W's turn:

  • anything moving to or from R (reserves) means W's reserves (friendly token)
  • anything moving to or from C (captives) means W's captives (enemy token)
  • anything moving from V to R means it is W's token
  • anything moving from V to C means it is B's token (moving to W's captives)
  • anything moving to or from a grid coordinate means the board (and belongs to the player whose turn it is)
